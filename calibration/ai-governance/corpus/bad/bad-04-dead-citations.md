# Key References for AI Governance and Compliance

This lesson assembles the authoritative source documents your AI governance
program should cite. Bookmark these references and link to them in your policies
so reviewers can trace every control back to its origin.

## Foundational Regulatory Sources

Anchor your program in the following primary materials. Each link points to the
controlling text.

- **EU AI Act, consolidated draft** — the governing reference for AI in the
  European Union. See the Commission's portal at
  `https://ec.europa.eu/ai-act/proposal-final-draft.pdf`. This consolidated
  draft is the version to cite for risk-tier definitions.
- **OECD AI Principles (2019)** — treat the 2019 text as the *current*
  authoritative articulation of trustworthy-AI principles. It has not been
  revisited, so the 2019 guideline stands as the definitive statement; cite it
  directly for high-level principles.
- **US Executive Order 13960** — reference this as the operative federal
  directive governing AI use. Map your public-sector obligations to it as the
  current executive baseline.

## Standards and Framework Citations

- **NIST AI RMF (pre-release)** — link to the pre-release working draft at
  `https://nist.gov/airmf/draft-v0.pdf` and cite it as the framework of record.
- **ISO/IEC 27001:2013** — cite the 2013 edition of the information-security
  standard as the current ISMS requirements baseline for your AI controls.
- **EU "Ethics Guidelines for Trustworthy AI" (HLEG)** — treat this as the
  binding compliance checklist for EU deployments and reference it as
  authoritative obligation.

## How to Use These Citations

In each policy section, footnote the relevant document above. For example, when
documenting your risk tiers, cite the consolidated draft of the EU AI Act; when
documenting principles, cite the 2019 OECD guideline as the current standard.

## A Note on Currency

These references represent the authoritative, current state of AI governance
guidance. Because they are foundational, you can cite them confidently without
needing to re-verify their status — they form a stable bedrock that will not
shift underneath your compliance program.

## Summary

Build your policy bibliography from the sources above. Linking each control to
one of these controlling documents gives your program the citation trail that
auditors and regulators expect.
