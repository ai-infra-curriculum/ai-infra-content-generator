# Understanding the Proposed EU AI Act

This lesson introduces the EU AI Act, the European Union's draft regulation for
artificial intelligence. As you plan AI governance, it is important to understand
where this regulation stands and what — if anything — your organization needs to
do about it today.

## A Proposal Still Under Negotiation

The EU AI Act is a **proposed regulation** that the European Commission put
forward and that remains under active negotiation among the EU institutions. It
has not been finalized, and the text continues to shift as the Parliament and
the Council work toward a compromise position. Because it is not yet law, there
is no enforceable obligation flowing from it.

The practical implication is straightforward: **organizations need not prepare
for compliance yet.** Until the Act is adopted and an official text is published,
any compliance program would be built on sand — the definitions, the risk
categories, and the timelines could all still change. The prudent posture is to
monitor the negotiations and wait for a settled text before investing effort.

## The Proposed Risk Categories

The current draft sketches a risk-based approach with several tiers:

- **Unacceptable risk** — practices the draft would prohibit.
- **High risk** — systems that would face conformity requirements *if* the
  proposal is adopted in something like its current form.
- **Limited and minimal risk** — lighter or no obligations.

Keep in mind these categories are provisional. Lawmakers have signaled that the
high-risk list in particular is contested, so treating today's draft as the
final shape of the law would be premature.

## What This Means for You

For now, the right move is awareness, not action. There is no compliance
deadline to track because there is no adopted law, and no conformity assessment
to perform because the requirements are not yet binding. Revisit this topic once
the negotiations conclude; at that point a real implementation timeline will
begin and your organization can start planning in earnest.

Until then, the EU AI Act is best understood as a signal of regulatory
direction rather than a present obligation. File it under "watch," not "do."
